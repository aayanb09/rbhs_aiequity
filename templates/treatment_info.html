Diabetes Treatment Information

Back to Tracker

Understanding how diabetes treatments work can help you make informed decisions about your health. This guide explains the main categories of diabetes medications and lifestyle interventions, how they work in your body, and what to expect from each treatment approach.

Diabetes management typically involves a combination of medication, lifestyle changes, and regular monitoring. Your healthcare provider will work with you to find the best treatment plan based on your type of diabetes, overall health, and personal preferences.

Important Medical Disclaimer This information is for educational purposes only and should not replace professional medical advice. Always consult with your healthcare provider before starting, stopping, or changing any medication. Never adjust your diabetes treatment without medical supervision.

Insulin Therapy

Insulin is a hormone that helps glucose enter your cells for energy. It's essential for Type 1 diabetes and sometimes needed for Type 2 diabetes.

Rapid-Acting Insulin Mealtime

How it works: Starts working within 15 minutes, peaks at 1 hour, and lasts 2-4 hours

  • Taken just before or with meals to cover the rise in blood glucose
  • Examples: Humalog, NovoLog, Apidra
  • Helps prevent post-meal glucose spikes

Long-Acting Insulin Basal

How it works: Provides steady glucose control throughout the day and night

  • Taken once or twice daily, usually at the same time
  • Examples: Lantus, Levemir, Tresiba
  • Keeps baseline glucose levels stable between meals

Insulin Pumps

How it works: Delivers small amounts of insulin continuously throughout the day

  • Mimics natural insulin production more closely
  • Allows for precise dosing adjustments
  • Can improve glucose control and flexibility

Oral Medications

Pills that help manage Type 2 diabetes by improving how your body uses insulin or produces glucose.

Metformin First-Line

How it works: Reduces glucose production in the liver and improves insulin sensitivity

  • Usually the first medication prescribed for Type 2 diabetes
  • Doesn't cause weight gain or low blood sugar when used alone
  • May cause mild digestive side effects initially

Sulfonylureas

How it works: Stimulates the pancreas to release more insulin

  • Examples: Glipizide, Glyburide, Glimepiride
  • Effective at lowering blood glucose quickly
  • Risk of hypoglycemia and weight gain

DPP-4 Inhibitors

How it works: Helps your body produce more insulin when needed and reduces glucose production

  • Examples: Januvia, Onglyza, Tradjenta
  • Low risk of hypoglycemia
  • Weight neutral

SGLT2 Inhibitors

How it works: Helps kidneys remove glucose through urine

  • Examples: Jardiance, Farxiga, Invokana
  • May help with weight loss and blood pressure
  • Protective benefits for heart and kidneys

Injectable Non-Insulin

Injectable medications that help control blood glucose through different mechanisms than insulin.

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists Popular

How it works: Mimics a natural hormone that increases insulin release, slows digestion, and reduces appetite

  • Examples: Ozempic, Trulicity, Victoza, Mounjaro
  • Often promotes weight loss
  • Cardiovascular benefits in many patients
  • Low risk of hypoglycemia when used alone

Amylin Analogs

How it works: Slows stomach emptying and reduces glucagon secretion

  • Example: Symlin (Pramlintide)
  • Used alongside mealtime insulin
  • Helps control post-meal glucose spikes

Combination Therapies

Medications that combine two different drugs in one pill or injection for convenience and enhanced effectiveness.

Fixed-Dose Combinations

How it works: Combines two medications with complementary mechanisms

  • Examples: Metformin + DPP-4 inhibitor, Metformin + SGLT2 inhibitor
  • Simplifies medication regimen
  • Attacks high blood glucose through multiple pathways

Why Combine Treatments?

How it works: Different mechanisms work together for better control

  • One medication alone may not achieve target glucose levels
  • Multiple pathways can provide more comprehensive control
  • May reduce side effects by using lower doses of each drug

Lifestyle Interventions

Lifestyle changes are the foundation of diabetes management and can be as powerful as medication in controlling blood glucose levels.

Nutrition & Diet

Carbohydrate counting, portion control, and choosing low glycemic index foods help maintain stable blood glucose. A balanced diet rich in vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains supports overall health.

Physical Activity

Regular exercise improves insulin sensitivity and helps muscles use glucose for energy. Aim for 150 minutes of moderate activity per week, including both aerobic exercise and strength training.

Weight Management

Losing even 5-10% of body weight can significantly improve blood glucose control, reduce medication needs, and lower risk of complications. Sustainable, gradual weight loss is most effective.

Sleep & Stress

Quality sleep (7-9 hours) and stress management are crucial. Poor sleep and chronic stress can raise cortisol levels, leading to insulin resistance and elevated blood glucose.

Blood Glucose Monitoring

Regular monitoring helps you understand how food, activity, stress, and medications affect your levels. This data guides treatment adjustments and empowers informed decisions.

Preventive Care

Regular check-ups, eye exams, foot care, and dental visits help catch and prevent complications early. Annual A1C tests, cholesterol checks, and kidney function tests are essential.
